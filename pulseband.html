Pulseband Dashboard

UI/Front-end Development

The Pulseband is a fictitious GE wearable product that enables users to track blood pressure and heart rate in hopes of reducing the risk of heart disease. I worked with another developer to design and create a responsive dashboard to log Pulseband data.

Exploring Pulseband

I spoke with a group of UX Designers who had interviewed doctors and individuals are at risk of heart disease. They informed me about their concerns and possible features that could address them. Some of the key features included:

  • Status updates to allow users to understand their general health without analyzing data.
  • Reminders to track medication and doctor's appointments.
  • Intuitive graphs to read at a glance.

Sprints

Within a 3 week constraint, My teammate and I worked through an agile methodology process, consisting of three main sprints:

  • Sprint 1: We reworked a previous mobile app design to create a web app dashboard.
  • Sprint 2: I worked with HTML/CSS & JavaScript to make a responsive interface while my teammate used d3.js to visualize the data from a JSON file.
  • Sprint 3: We both used AngularJS to populate data onto the dashboard, debugged, and finalized our code.

Sprint 1: Design Process

We used a mobile app design made by a previous team of UX/UI designers. These designs were based on user research and competitive analyses.

Style Tile

We made a quick style tile to get an idea of the colors/branding of our dashboard.

Sketches

We made some rough sketches of the dashboard layout. We organized content and data based on the mobile app design.

Final Designs

We created these final designs after sketching.

Sprint 2: HTML/CSS/JS/D3.js

I primarily worked with HTML/CSS/JS to create a responsive dashboard, health data, profile, and settings page while my teammate worked to visualize data using d3.js.Some of the issues we came across was making our graphs responsive and creating a time object in which the graphs would update when the user changed the date in the date-picker.

Sprint 3: AngularJS / Debugging

We both populated the dashboard with all the necessary elements using AngularJS. We used Angular's routing library, NG route, to dynamically link all the pages of the dashboard without reloading the page. We also utilized multiple Ng-repeats and filters to access our JSON data and generate web app's features.
